The Quiet Art of Designing Systems That Last

EN
Elena Navarro
Feb 12, 2026 8 min read

Introduction

Good design systems don't announce themselves. They recede into the background, providing just enough structure for teams to move quickly without second-guessing every decision. The best ones feel inevitable in hindsight — not because they were obvious, but because they earned trust one small choice at a time.

Rebuilding from Scratch

When we started rebuilding our component library eighteen months ago, the temptation was to over-specify everything. Every spacing value, every color variant, every interaction state documented down to the pixel. We'd seen other teams do this and admired the thoroughness. But thoroughness without restraint becomes its own kind of debt.

A system that tries to anticipate every need will satisfy none of them well. Constraints should guide, not imprison.

Choosing Primitives

Instead, we focused on a smaller surface area — a handful of primitives that could compose into more complex patterns. A card is just a container with consistent padding and a border. A badge is just a small label with a background. Nothing clever, nothing surprising. The power comes from how predictably they combine.

Composition over Configuration

Rather than adding props for every variant, we leaned into composition. A status indicator is just a badge inside a card header. A user row is an avatar next to a text block. When the primitives are consistent, you don't need fifty specialized components — you need five good ones and the freedom to arrange them.

The Role of Friction

There's a counterintuitive truth about design systems: a little friction is healthy. If every component is trivially easy to use, people stop thinking about whether they should use it. We intentionally left some patterns uncodified — not because we couldn't build them, but because the conversation around "should we add this?" was more valuable than the component itself.

The RFC Process

We introduced a lightweight RFC process for new components. Any team member can propose an addition, but they need to document three real use cases and show that existing primitives can't solve the problem. This has cut our component count by a third while increasing satisfaction scores from the teams using the system.

Governance and Adoption

A design system without governance is just a component library that nobody trusts. We established a rotating council of two designers and two engineers who review proposals weekly. Decisions are documented publicly, and every rejection includes a rationale. Transparency builds trust faster than any onboarding deck.

Measuring Success

We track three metrics: adoption rate across product teams, time-to-first-component for new engineers, and the ratio of custom overrides to standard usage. The last one is the most telling — when teams stop overriding your components, you know the system is working.
